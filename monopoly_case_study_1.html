Background

How Microsoft Built Its Empire

In 1981, IBM selected Microsoft's MS-DOS as the operating system for its personal computer. Unlike typical licensing deals, Microsoft retained the right to sell MS-DOS to other manufacturers — a decision that would transform the software landscape forever.

As IBM-compatible "clone" PCs flooded the market through the 1980s, every machine needed an operating system. Microsoft — and only Microsoft — had the dominant product. By the time Windows 95 launched, Microsoft had achieved something rare in economic history: a near-total monopoly over the foundational software layer of a global technology platform.

The company's strategy was brilliant: it wasn't just selling software. It was controlling a bottleneck. Every software application needed to run on an operating system, and that operating system was Windows.

The Network Effect Trap: The more users adopted Windows, the more developers wrote applications for Windows. The more applications existed for Windows, the more users chose Windows. This self-reinforcing cycle — known as a network effect — made it nearly impossible for competitors to break in, even with superior products.

This created what economists call barriers to entry — structural advantages that prevent competitors from challenging the incumbent. For Microsoft, these barriers were enormous: switching costs for users, application compatibility lock-in, and the sheer cost of convincing developers to abandon Windows for a new platform.

Key Insight: Microsoft's monopoly was not just about market share numbers. It was about structural power — the ability to define the rules of an entire industry.
Timeline

A Monopoly in Motion

1981
The IBM Deal — Foundation of an Empire
Microsoft licenses MS-DOS to IBM but retains rights to sell to others. This single clause would make Bill Gates the richest person on Earth.
1990
Windows 3.0 — Mass Market Conquest
Windows 3.0 sells over 3 million copies in its first year. Microsoft begins its ascent to PC operating system dominance with a genuinely usable graphical interface.
1995
Windows 95 & The Browser Wars Begin
Windows 95 launches to massive fanfare. Microsoft notices that Netscape Navigator — a browser — is threatening to become an alternative platform. The Browser Wars begin.
1996–98
Internet Explorer Bundling — The Anticompetitive Act
Microsoft bundles Internet Explorer with Windows for free and pressures PC manufacturers not to pre-install Netscape. Netscape's market share collapses from 80% to under 20%.
1998
U.S. Department of Justice Files Antitrust Suit
20 state attorneys general and the DOJ sue Microsoft under the Sherman Antitrust Act, alleging monopolistic business practices that harmed competition and consumers.
2000
Judge Orders Microsoft to Break Up
Judge Jackson rules Microsoft must be split into two companies — one for Windows, one for software products. Microsoft appeals immediately.
2001
Settlement — The Monopoly Survives, Constrained
The Bush administration settles with Microsoft. The breakup is cancelled. Instead, Microsoft must share its APIs with third parties and accept oversight. Critics call it a victory for Microsoft.
Economic Analysis

Monopoly Theory Applied to Microsoft

Economic Theory

A pure monopoly exists when a single firm is the sole seller of a product with no close substitutes. The firm faces the entire downward-sloping market demand curve, making it a price maker rather than a price taker.

Profit maximisation occurs where MR = MC. Because Marginal Revenue lies below the demand curve (each additional unit sold requires a price cut on all units), the monopolist produces less than the competitive output and charges a higher price, generating economic (supernormal) profit.

The gap between the competitive equilibrium and the monopoly equilibrium represents deadweight loss — a welfare cost borne by society from unrealised transactions.

How Microsoft Priced Like a Monopolist

Windows OEM licensing fees were set well above marginal cost (which for software approaches zero after development). In the 1990s, Microsoft charged PC makers $40–$80 per Windows license — for software that cost virtually nothing to replicate.

This is textbook monopoly pricing: P >> MC, generating enormous supernormal profit. Microsoft's operating margins on Windows exceeded 85% in peak years — a figure impossible in competitive markets.

Furthermore, Microsoft used "per-processor" licensing — charging manufacturers a fee for every PC sold, regardless of whether it ran Windows. This effectively taxed any manufacturer who dared install a competing OS.

Barriers to Entry — Why Rivals Couldn't Win

Network Effects
Windows' value grew with every new user and application — a self-reinforcing moat.
Switching Costs
Businesses invested billions in Windows-compatible software — making switching prohibitively expensive.
Predatory Pricing
IE was offered free — deliberately below cost — to destroy Netscape's revenue model.
Exclusive Dealing
PC makers were pressured (via incentives/threats) not to feature rival browsers or operating systems.

Welfare Impact

Who Won, Who Lost?

Costs to Society

Deadweight Loss: Fewer consumers accessed certain software products (especially browsers) because competition was artificially suppressed. The market produced less variety than it would have under competition.

Innovation Suppressed: Netscape's death delayed browser development. Java was hamstrung. Economists estimate the browser stagnation of 2001–2004 (IE's unchallenged dominance post-Netscape) cost years of web innovation.

Higher Prices: Windows licensing costs were passed on to consumers in the price of every PC — with no competitive pressure to reduce them.

Benefits Acknowledged

Standardisation: A single dominant OS platform enabled a consistent environment for software development — reducing fragmentation costs that plagued earlier computing.

Scale Economies: Microsoft's monopoly profits funded enormous R&D. Office, SQL Server, and Exchange — widely adopted enterprise tools — emerged from this investment.

Consumer Access: The commoditisation of the PC — aided by cheap Windows licensing to volume manufacturers — contributed to rapid diffusion of computers into homes and businesses.

Verdict: The court found that the costs — suppressed competition, harmed rivals, slower innovation — outweighed the benefits. Microsoft was ruled a monopolist who had abused its power.
Legacy & Lessons

What Microsoft's Case Teaches Us

The Microsoft case remains the most important antitrust action in the technology industry's history. It established that:

① Market power + exclusionary conduct = antitrust liability, even for companies that achieved dominance through innovation.

② Bundling can be predatory when done specifically to leverage one monopoly into another market.

③ Network effects create natural monopoly tendencies in technology platforms — requiring proactive regulatory attention.

The case's settlement — widely criticised as too lenient — nonetheless changed Microsoft's culture and strategy. The company became less aggressive, opened APIs, and stopped bundling in ways that leveraged Windows dominance.

Ironically, Microsoft's constraints opened space for Google, Mozilla Firefox, and Apple Safari to reshape the browser market — and ultimately for smartphones (iOS and Android) to break Windows' grip on the computing platform entirely.

Today's Echo: Regulators are applying the Microsoft precedent to Google (Search), Apple (App Store), and Meta (Social Media) — making this case a living template for 21st-century antitrust law.
